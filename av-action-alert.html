AV Action Alert

Now is the time for disabled advocates and their allies to speak up loudly about the need for accessibility requirements in any legislation permitting automated vehicles in DC.

Speak up now

Demand accessible AVs

Please see more background on the issue, recommended advocacy actions, and suggested messaging that people can use for testifying or submitting comments. Share this far and wide so that we can get the word out.

Happy to answer questions and be a resource on this effort. Email info@maacdc.org with any questions. Thanks for your support!

Background

Why this matters

A bill has been proposed in the DC Council that will allow automated vehicles (AVs) to operate and provide passenger service in DC. They will operate like a taxi or ride share service. While AVs have the potential to expand transportation options for people with disabilities in DC, it will only work if the service is accessible for people across the disability spectrum (blind, Deaf, cognitive, mobility, and so forth). Right now, no AV company has an accessible vehicle and the proposed legislation in DC does not mandate accessibility for disabled people. The time to demand accessibility is now, not after inaccessible vehicles are on the road.

Messages

What to say

Suggested messaging that people can use for testifying or submitting comments.

  • DC must mandate that automated vehicles are accessible for ALL people with disabilities.
  • Failing to do so violates both the DC Human Rights Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act because it will result in clear discrimination against disabled people.

Four requirements must be added to legislation permitting AV deployment for it to be effective in ensuring accessibility for disabled riders:

That the vehicles are wheelchair accessible for loading and securement.

That the vehicles can communicate and be controlled by riders simply and effectively in multiple ways — through an accessible app, audio, and visual cues, and so forth.

That it can detect and interact appropriately with disabled pedestrians (blind, Deaf, wheelchair users, people with intellectual and developmental disabilities, and so forth).

That it protects the safety of both disabled riders and pedestrians, including people with darker skin tones and gender non-conforming people.

Permitting AV companies to “refer to an outside service” for accessibility is wrong, doesn’t work, and would strain the already extremely limited accessible transportation currently operating.
